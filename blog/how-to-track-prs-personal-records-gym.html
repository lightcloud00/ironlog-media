How to Track PRs (Personal Records) in the Gym

Published May 23, 2026 · 6 min read

Every lifter — from the first day in the gym to the veteran chasing a 500-pound deadlift — tracks PRs the same way: some number went up. Weight on the bar, reps at a weight, or both. That's a PR. But how you track it matters more than most people realize.

Tracking PRs well means you can see your trajectory, know when to push harder, and know when to back off. Here's how to do it right.

What Counts as a PR

Most people think PR only means "new max weight." But there are actually a few different kinds:

  • Weight PR — lifted a heavier weight than ever before (e.g., bench 225 lbs for the first time)
  • Rep PR — got more reps at a given weight than ever before (e.g., 10 reps at 185 lbs when your previous max was 8)
  • Volume PR — total reps × weight in a session or exercise was higher than any previous session
  • Bodyweight PR — hit a rep target at a lower bodyweight (for bodyweight exercises like pull-ups)

All of these are legitimate progress signals. The key is picking what you're actually chasing and tracking it consistently.

Why Most People Don't Track PRs Well

The usual approach: you remember your "best bench" because it was a big day. But memory is unreliable. Six months later you think you hit 225 but the reality was 215 — and now you're chasing a ghost number.

The other problem: most apps make PR tracking complicated. You have to dig into history, manually compare sessions, or — in the case of some apps — pay to see your own PR data.

The fix is simple: log every set, every session. Let the app surface the PRs for you.

How to Track PRs Effectively

1. Log Every Set, Every Session

No exceptions. PRs hide in consistency. You can't find a PR you didn't log.

Use an app that makes logging fast — tap in, enter weight and reps, move on. IronLog is built around a quick set-entry flow so logging never slows your session down.

2. Pick a Tracking Method

There are two approaches:

Exercise-specific PRs: Track your estimated 1RM for compound lifts (squat, bench, deadlift, OHP). Use a calculator — most apps have one built in. Your estimated 1RM is the weight you'd theoretically lift for 1 rep based on your performance at higher reps.

Straight-up recorded maxes: For accessory work, just record the actual weight and reps. If it beats your previous best at that exercise, it's a PR.

3. Review Before Each Session

This is the step most people skip. Before your next bench day, look at:

  • What was your last bench session weight/reps?
  • Did you hit a PR last time?
  • What's the target for today?

4. Celebrate and Record

Hit a PR? Log it. Write it down. Take a photo of the bar if it matters to you. This is data worth preserving.

Common PR Tracking Mistakes

Chasing only 1RMs. Lifting to true maxes every week is a fast track to injury. Most of your training should be submaximal — and PRs at 3-5 reps count just as much.

Not tracking volume. If you benched 225×5 last month and 225×8 today, that's massive progress even if the weight didn't go up. Track reps at weight, not just maxes.

Skipping the small PRs. A 2.5-pound jump on a lat pulldown still counts. Compound interest applies to lifting: small PRs add up.

Ignoring recovery. PRs only happen if you recover. Sleep, protein, and programming matter as much as tracking.
