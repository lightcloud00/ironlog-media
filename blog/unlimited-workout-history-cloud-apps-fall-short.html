Unlimited Workout History: Why Cloud Apps Fall Short

Published May 23, 2026 · 5 min read

Here's a scenario that plays out constantly in gyms everywhere:

You start tracking your workouts in an app. Maybe it's Hevy, maybe it's Strong. You get invested — six months in, you love the data, you love seeing your progress. Then you decide you don't want to pay $2.99/month anymore. Or you just forget to update your card. And then you find out: your workout history is gone.

Hevy's free tier wipes your log after 3 months. Strong's free tier caps you at 3 routines. In both cases, the cloud model means your data lives on their servers — and their servers can take it away.

This isn't a fringe complaint. It's a fundamental problem with how most fitness apps are built.

The Cloud Storage Problem

Cloud-first apps store your workout data on remote servers. That sounds convenient — your data syncs across devices, you can log from your phone and check stats on your laptop. But it creates a power imbalance.

When your data lives in the cloud:

  • The company controls access to it
  • They can limit it (3 months, 4 routines, etc.) to force upgrades
  • If the company shuts down, changes pricing, or gets acquired, your history is on their terms
  • You need an account just to start
  • Offline access is an afterthought — good luck logging in a basement gym with no signal

The business model matters here. Free apps with cloud storage need to convert free users to paid users somehow. History paywalls are one of the most effective conversion tools they have. "You can't see your gains from January — but for $2.99/month..." It's frustrating by design.

What "Unlimited History" Actually Means

When an app says "unlimited history," it should mean exactly that: every session you've ever logged is available to you, forever, without conditions. No expiry. No paywall. No "upgrade to see your 2024 data."

This is how IronLog is built. Workout history lives on your device, locally. Your training log is yours. IronLog doesn't require an account to use the core tracker — you download it, you open it, you start logging. No email, no password, no terms-of-service maze to get through.

The tradeoff: local storage means your data stays on your phone. That's actually a feature for most people. Your gym training log is personal data that doesn't need to be on a server. It travels with your device. It doesn't require an internet connection to access.

Local-First vs. Cloud Storage

Here's the honest comparison:

Neither model is objectively wrong. Cloud sync is genuinely useful if you want your data on multiple devices. But for most people training consistently at the same gym, local-first storage is actually the better fit — your phone is already with you, it works in any basement gym with no signal, and your data is fully under your control.

The Accountability Gap

There's a psychological angle here too. When your history is locked behind a paywall, you lose the accountability that comes from seeing it. Six months of consistent training, right there in your phone, is motivating. But if that history disappears the moment you stop paying, the app is taking something from you that should be yours.

Local history keeps you honest. You can scroll back a year and see exactly where you were. That bench press you PR'd last March? Still there. That leg session you skipped three times in a row? All logged. The data is yours, which means the accountability is yours too.

Data Portability Matters

One more thing cloud apps often bury: export. If you decide to leave, can you take your data with you?

IronLog's free plan includes CSV export. Every set you've logged, every session, in a format you own. Spreadsheet it, import it into another app, back it up to iCloud yourself. Your training data, your format, your choice.

Some cloud apps only let you export with a paid subscription — which means you're paying again just to access your own history in a portable format. That's backwards.

The Real Cost of "Free" Cloud Apps

Let's do the math on the cloud apps:

  • Hevy: $2.99/month = $35.88/year to keep your full history
  • Strong: $4.99/month = $59.88/year for full features and history

Over five years, that's $179–$300 spent just to maintain access to your own training data. Meanwhile, IronLog keeps your history on-device, free, forever.

The apps aren't really free if you're paying with your data — and your history is more valuable than a subscription fee.
